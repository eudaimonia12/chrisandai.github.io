# Why I Study Both Philosophy and Code

People often raise an eyebrow when they learn my path: a Bachelor of Philosophy from
Nanjing University, then a Master of Information Technology in Auckland. The two sound
like they belong to different worlds — one abstract and ancient, the other concrete and
new. I've come to believe they're the same discipline wearing different clothes.

## Both start with a question

Philosophy taught me to distrust the obvious. Before answering a question, you first
have to ask whether it's the *right* question. Software is no different. The hardest part
of building the AWS AI claims platform wasn't the model or the API — it was deciding what
"a correct claim" even meant, and where a machine's confidence should end and a human's
judgement should begin.

> Good engineering, like good philosophy, is mostly the art of asking a sharper question.

## Logic is logic

A well-formed argument and a well-formed program share a spine. Premises become inputs,
inference becomes control flow, and a contradiction is just a failing test you haven't
written yet. Years of untangling arguments made me comfortable sitting with a bug until
its structure revealed itself.

## Clarity is a form of respect

In sales and market development I learned that an idea only matters if the person across
the table understands it. Philosophy trains you to say difficult things plainly;
engineering forces you to make them *work*. Between the two, I try to build things that
are both true and legible.
