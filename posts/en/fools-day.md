On the morning of April 1st the rain had stopped, but the ground was not yet dry. The little lane in front of the house, usually smooth and level, betrayed its flaws in the rain: potholes large and small turned into pools of water that reflected the pallid sky without expression. If she walked to school, she would surely pretend to step carelessly into the water and soak her socks and new shoes. She is the kind of child who draws a sharp line between the reckless act and its unhappy consequence, and lays the whole of the latter at my feet. And so I had no choice but to face the cold, damp outdoor air, lift her onto the back seat, and make my voice sound as patient as possible: "There's a good girl — Grandpa will ride you to school this morning."

I hadn't ridden this bicycle for a while, but happily it ran without trouble. A bicycle is exactly the sort of tool that, through time, is always worth your trust. As the wheels rolled forward, the dim light of the dynamo hub began to appear, then grew bright, and at that moment I remembered the year I first got it. Back then, the computer virus that appeared at the end of '19 had already invaded 99.9% of the world's networked devices, and the connected world people took for granted no longer existed. No matter the communication protocol, no matter the underlying system or code, that virus — able to spread across any local network — could always gain a foothold in the hardware and release cryptic instructions that burned the chips into a puddle of sludge. People later became certain that it had first lodged itself in a fifteen-second short video. A few hours after, when the idol in the video publicly apologized for his widely circulated discriminatory remarks, there were few left who could receive his message. Or any other message. I don't care for entertainment; the day it happened, I took a wrong turn because my navigation had stopped updating, and as dusk closed in I suddenly realized I was already two hundred kilometers from my destination, with no way to communicate with any charging station.

It really did take a while for humankind to overcome its internet phantom limb. Most people had been dismissive before, thinking internet addiction had nothing to do with them. In the end, nearly everyone I knew went through nausea, exhaustion, insomnia, and binge eating, followed by a long emptiness; some even lost their voice, their sight, or their hearing. The senses had lost their objects; sweet information had become a luxury. The internet went extinct with astonishing speed, like the once-ubiquitous passenger pigeon. Energetic youths fell first; the lives of the old went untouched. People stared, at a loss, at screens that would never light up again, hoping the person in the black mirror could teach them how to take up living once more. There is no counting how many died because of the virus. Of course, there were parts worth singing of, too: universities, research institutes, and computer-industry professionals across the globe formed a dream team, and billions of devices were donated to shoulder the computing load. They resisted day and night from their corner, keeping in contact through Cold War–era encrypted intranets. People tried to translate the virus's code, a task no easier than picking out an enemy's shadow in the noonday sun. Large-scale virus analysis ended in 2022, when an unprecedented depression swept everything away. To this day, only the independent research groups in Boao and Kyiv remain operational, falling ever further behind the growth in the virus's complexity and diversity — all of which she will read about in her seventh-grade history textbook. In my own memory, that stretch of time actually passed quickly. I had always kept a keep-your-distance relationship with contemporary technology; the hardest part was losing a few important friends. At first I was a postman, and bought this bicycle for the work. Later I taught a public-writing class in the community, and met her grandmother in the tea room. I had no plans for my life at all, until one afternoon we swung together on the swings and she quoted a line from a Russian writer: "Love with all your heart, and leave the rest to fate."

We passed a garden of tulips in full bloom, their curved green leaves looking comical, dozens of yellow flowers standing upright like a crowd of happy fools. She begged me to dig one up and plant it on the balcony, though she had already killed several potted plants. I said that if she wanted to grow flowers she would have to touch the soil with her own hands. She agreed indifferently, and I decided to go to the library that afternoon to borrow a book on gardening and read it with her. I can't make out this child's talent, because she is full of talent for everything. I'll bet anything that reading and labor will make her one of the finest of her generation.

We greeted the mild-tempered, slightly built security guard, and she skipped through the school gate; I called out loudly not to forget to bring her lunchbox home. And how shall I spend the rest of the day? When I was young I used to ride the nearby forest trails with my companions; now, I'll go there to gather mushrooms after the rain.
