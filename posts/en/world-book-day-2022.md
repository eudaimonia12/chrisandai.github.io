Yesterday I pulled the 2012 edition of *Written on Water* down from the very bottom shelf to read again. I bought this collection of Eileen Chang's essays back in high school, and ever since, every year or so, I take it out and leaf through it. I once carried it with me to Nanjing and for a while assumed I had lost it somewhere in the past decade. Fumbling around during lockdown, I found it again without the slightest effort.

I love the way this edition is bound; I love the unevenness of chapters that run long and short; I love the subtle sensation at my fingertips as I stroke the pages that have begun to yellow. The collection gathers the essays Eileen Chang published in Shanghai magazines between 1940 and 1947, on subjects ranging from ordinary street life to urban observation to literary criticism. Many of the details of Hong Kong and Shanghai in the film *Lust, Caution* come not from the original novella but are stitched together from fragments of these essays. In the past, my pleasure in reading came from her acute feeling and her flashes of genius; I paid no attention to her tireless descriptions of petty-bourgeois life. Now, in a time of upheaval, rereading the very passages I once thought "fussy and sentimental," a fresh sense of recognition rises unbidden. I can nod along even to lines like these:

> "Human beings are born meddlers. Why shouldn't we sneak a glance into one another's private lives, when the one being watched loses little and the watcher clearly gains a moment's pleasure? In anything that touches on the giving and receiving of happiness, it isn't worth splitting hairs. What is there to weigh against what? — Long is the tribulation, short is life."

> "Perhaps this is just what life is. To be alive is to be troubled; and if you dread the trouble, you may as well be dead. The moment the trouble is over, the person is over too."

The essays record the Shanghai of the 1940s under Japanese occupation, and count as first-hand historical material. By comparison, apart from the absence of the risk of dropping dead in the street and a markedly improved standard of mere survival, the condition of the city today is scarcely different. Falling ill and seeing a doctor is not allowed; going home to cook dinner is not allowed; discussing politics is, of course, not allowed either. That being so, no amount of ink spent on the small joys of a mediocre life is excessive. The everyday matters of eating and sleeping glow; the neighborly relations of upstairs and downstairs matter enormously. I have come to this realization too — one who has never lived through suffering cannot possibly read suffering. Only, this kind of realization is better not had at all.

Since a person can only read from a book what they already know, now is the golden age for reading Sartre, Kafka, and Eileen Chang. When chaos and violence come knocking at the door, hiding in the bookcase works better than hiding under the bed.

The point of this day is reading, not writing, so I'll end with a quotation too:

> "In this age, the old is collapsing and the new is still growing. But before the high tide of the age arrives, anything clear-cut and decisive is only the exception. People merely feel that everything in daily life is somehow off — off to the point of terror. A person lives within an age, yet the age is sinking away like a shadow, and one feels oneself abandoned. To confirm one's own existence, to grasp something real and elementary, one cannot but turn to ancient memory — the memory of all the ages that humankind has lived through, which is clearer and more intimate than any gazing into the future."
