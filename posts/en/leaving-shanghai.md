## I

After eleven and a half hours in the air, I returned to my hometown, nine thousand kilometers away. The two years that separated us, the half-day time difference, the vast ocean, a whole stretch of life — all of it was thrown behind me the moment I set foot on solid concrete. The biting air, the familiar gravity; yet my steps were heavier than I'd imagined, a reminder that what had happened was no dream, the lightness of gait I had lost being proof enough.

In the past, my parents would take me abroad during the Spring Festival holiday. Every time we returned to Pudong Airport, the impression was always humid and stifling, even in winter. In those years our destinations were mostly at higher northern latitudes, which made Shanghai seem warm by comparison. But returning this time from New Zealand, I felt only an irresistible dank cold. My checked luggage was a single travel bag, yet I waited half an hour for it — probably because the throughput of people at Pudong far exceeds that of luggage. By the time I walked out of customs it was already midnight, Auckland time.

Worn out from the journey, with the stars wheeling overhead. By custom, one should eat a McDonald's on landing.

This was like a homecoming delayed by two years. Nine hundred days earlier I had set out for New Zealand on a tourist visa, and was stopped at the check-in counter because I'd bought a one-way ticket. Clearly this was a journey with no return; in order to board, I bought on the spot a return ticket for the following March, up to the stay limit of the tourist visa, and refunded it after landing. From then on, my steps were confined to one corner of the South Pacific.

Two years later, on roughly the same day in early March, I returned to my hometown. To say that "home is where someone waits for you at the airport" is not wrong, but megacities like Beijing and Shanghai may be the exception. A round trip by car takes more than three hours. So there was no car waiting for me. Having sat rigid for a dozen hours, I was in no hurry to hail a cab, and instead hopped onto the metro in the cold wind; the train sped westward and I felt a surge of exhilaration. A pity that after a few stops my passion was spent, replaced by fatigue and boredom. I got off at Longyang Road, a transfer station that also has a McDonald's, where I could eat my fill before deciding how to travel next.

The grilled chicken-thigh burger was the first delicacy my girlfriend and I had agreed to taste. New Zealand's McDonald's ranks among the worst in the world, offering only a few of the most basic and cheapest burgers, the chicken, beef, pork, and fish all monotonous patties. By comparison, the grilled-chicken burger made for the domestic market is tender and flavorful, a shared delicious memory, and we both missed it.

The Longyang Road McDonald's at half past eight in the evening was like a coaching inn: the mealtime had passed, but there were no fewer customers, people like me who were idle and exhausted; here our bodies and innards caught their breath and took on low-quality provisions. Perhaps I'd expected too much, but the first McDonald's failed to meet expectations: the chicken tasted "chickeny," proof it wasn't fresh or wasn't fully cooked; the fries were barely warm, limp and shapeless. Since this meal couldn't lift my spirits, and I had no wish to keep dragging my luggage along, I took a taxi from Pudong back to Baoshan.

I tried to make out the familiar landmarks. Taking the Jungong Road tunnel onto the Middle Ring Road was once my daily commute, yet on this night I couldn't make sense of it at all. Time slips by in an unfamiliar city; only after passing Yangpu did I suddenly realize this was not the road I knew. It turned out that Jungong Road — for decades bumpy, rutted, and dust-blown — had been rebuilt into a new interchange, vehicles glowing with faint blue lights racing along the lanes, the red and blue taillights woven together under the night sky like the exhaust of spacecraft. Asking a friend, I learned that the blue lights are a new generation of signal lights, meaning the vehicles are in autonomous mode. The spread of self-driving is no surprise, but the blue lights make it visible, and at once I felt science fiction had walked into reality. The completion of the new interchange not only improved conditions on Jungong Road but also shortened the distance one must travel on the elevated Yixian Road. Outside the window was no longer Metro Line 3, but wharves, warehouses, and cargo ships. The dozen-odd kilometers of rubble along Yixian Road separate Yangpu from Baoshan, a chasm that cannot be shaken; but Shanghai always has some new way of vaulting over it.

## II

I came back this time out of longing for my grandparents' generation.

I too was once smitten with this city. What I loved, of course, was the romanticized image — the bustling streets and markets woven from the words of Lu Xun and Eileen Chang, Wang Anyi and Jin Yucheng. In my imagination, morning drew back its curtain amid the sound of ship whistles, the fragrance of dim sum hung in the air, and in the gesture of pulling open the curtains there was strength and hope; deep in the plane-tree-shaded streets lay a labyrinth of longtang alleys, where no one could wake the languid, endless afternoon; when the city's lights first came on, men and women dressed to the nines for their rendezvous; and late at night the Huangpu gently stroked its banks, confiding a century of rumor and legend. I could only fall in love with Shanghai through the words of others. Reality is nothing but a forever gloomy sky, tangled knots of metro lines, one ever-changing shopping mall after another, one interchange after another radiating in all directions, where — green light or red — the electric scooters dive like a kamikaze corps, where the gorgeous neon has been replaced by glaring LED screens, a flawless face that up close shatters into pixels, meaningless banners hung on utterly needless walls, without the faintest trace of humanity. In the dark of night the black towers stand in rows, the metro's roar passing through their midst; against such a scene, the most brilliant tale of a foreign land, once told, turns insipid. What can it matter that you left, and came back? Nothing but a pebble tossed into a deep pool.
