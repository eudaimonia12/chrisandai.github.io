One of Eileen Chang's short stories is titled "Steamed Osmanthus: Ah Xiao's Autumn Sorrow," and tells of a single day in the life of a maidservant who waits on a foreign man. It is extraordinarily moving. The maidservant is named Ah Xiao, and her wistfulness and vexation are the "autumn sorrow"; but though I've read it three times over the years, I never understood what "steamed osmanthus" meant. I only felt the phrase was lovely to look at, pleasing to say, full of lingering flavor. Unfortunately there was no annotation, and I never had the mind to look it up.

It appears only once, at the very beginning, where the author quotes a good friend: "Autumn is a song, but the night of 'steamed osmanthus' is like a xiao's tune played in the kitchen; the daytime is like a song sung by children — hot and ripe, clear and moist all at once." It leaves one baffled.

Today I understood. Steamed osmanthus refers to the climate of those few days in early autumn when the osmanthus is fragrant and the warmth returns. Humid and sultry, with an intoxicating scent everywhere. Just like Shanghai at this very moment, back above thirty degrees, with even the cicada song — silent for some time — sounding again. The mosquitoes, too, have multiplied. After the steamed osmanthus comes the final farewell to summer, and from then on it cools irrevocably. This way of putting it is far gentler than "autumn tiger."
