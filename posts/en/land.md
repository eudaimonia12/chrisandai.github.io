During this Shanghai outbreak, I fell into a similar musing: the fate of the Jewish people is to wander forever without a home, driven out, trampled, and enslaved again and again, yet still doggedly seeking the promised land. The Chinese, then, have fallen under an opposite curse — their bond with the land and with farming is so overwhelmingly strong that free migration and pioneering became, both economically and conceptually, no longer feasible, and a deep-rooted feudal system arose accordingly. Marriage, family, patriarchy, clan, and state interlock link by link into a chain of cause and effect, sustaining the stability and order of agrarian society and together driving the productive machine of farming civilization. The foremost feature of this machine has never changed: it is, as *A Madman's Diary* put it, "eating people." Man tills the land, and the land tills man; man is at once the productive force and the means of production. In the end, man discovers that there is, after all, not much difference between himself and the crops he tills.

This is not merely the problem of you, or me, or some group "more responsible for it than others"; it is a complex and universal predicament that runs through the whole of human history. That being so, rather than the omnipresent irony, thinking about and attempting alternatives is of more use in improving our own lives and each other's. Those alternatives are all touched on in this essay: the natural environment need not be harsh; agricultural production is not obligatory; farming is not necessarily "advanced"; the linear "march of civilization" very possibly does not exist; and history has more than one or two ways of being narrated. Only by daring to imagine can we create change.
