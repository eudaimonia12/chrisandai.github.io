Throughout this period of study I have often felt empty, anxious, and adrift — feelings unfamiliar to me, which have forced me out of a comfort zone I had settled into with an easy conscience. The reasons are nothing out of the ordinary:

First, I am living on "bought time" in an unfamiliar environment, where language, culture, and the color of my skin are all chasms.

Second, the world I live in is no longer on any settled course; from politics and the economy at the largest scale down to career planning at the smallest, there is no certainty to speak of.

Third, what I study is neither what I love nor what I am good at — at best it amounts to "not averse to it, able to do it, and doing it not badly."

Fourth, I lack influence and the power to change my surroundings: within, no rights and no freedom; without, no connections and no standing.

Fifth, I have no stable or sufficient income; the meager wages of odd jobs cannot reverse the predicament of eating into what little I have.

Of course, as a rational person, I have good reason to believe that all of the above will be duly resolved within a cycle of three to five years. But distant water cannot quench a present thirst; the dawn on the horizon brings no warmth. Worry not only degrades the quality of life, it also drives one to desperate decisions.

For now, the best remedy is to read literature, especially good novels. Lately I've been reading Kazuo Ishiguro in English and Li Zishu in Chinese — the others I can't manage — and I've still found much consolation. Like idle talk at a bar, the words pour out like liquor; but neither alcohol nor words are a remedy. Other people's lives are.
