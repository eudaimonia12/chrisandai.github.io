*Commentary and apologia*

In this "polemical work," Nietzsche traces the origin of morality (which he more often calls "value judgment"); and the aim of the tracing is to pass a value judgment upon "value judgment" itself. By revealing the genealogy of its "slave morality," Nietzsche, in an anti-ethical posture, evaluates and critiques Christian values.

Standing on this step into modernity that is the nineteenth century, people face the condition of "God is dead": willing or not, they have walked out of the sheltering valley of God onto a broad, unattended plain — this is the world of the grown adult, who must survive here on his own. Like the person in Plato's allegory of the cave who comes out into the sunlight, his intellect attains an unprecedented satisfaction, he draws step by step nearer to truth, step by step realizes himself; and once in a state of conscious striving, he can no longer willingly return to ignorance and self-deception. The person moving toward modernity has likewise bidden farewell to that medieval haven in the bosom of God. But at this point the farewell is not yet complete: the constraint of church and dogma upon him has lightened, but Christianity has already carved its deepest mark in his heart in the form of morality. In unreflective moments he not only makes value judgments according to Christian morality, but bases all his conduct upon it; and when he does reflect, he confines the object of his crusade to the church: "If it were not for the church, who among us would still want to be a freethinker? It is against our church that we rebel, not against its poison… Apart from the church, we still love this poison…" People representing the advanced set oppose the external authority-institution that constrains them, yet relish the internalized form of that authority, that "poisoning of the spirit": Christian morality remains the substrate of life. Nineteenth-century people strove to shake off faith, yet clung to the notions of good and evil rooted in that faith, like an eye grown used to the dark that cannot bear a sudden light. And so, under these historical conditions, Christian morality has become an obstacle to humanity's great strides forward; and so, someone discovered this obstacle, and loudly called for a revaluation of the moral tradition that had reigned for nearly two thousand years; and so, the hidden side of morality — "morality as consequence, symptom, mask, hypocrisy, sickness, misunderstanding; but also as cause, as remedy, stimulant, inhibitor, poison" — was brought to light under Nietzsche's pen.

As an excellent classical scholar, Nietzsche traces the genealogy of morality from the standpoint of etymology. In Greek, Latin, and German, the ethical categories of "good" and "bad," as well as "good" and "evil," derive respectively from "noble" and "base." "Noble" and "lofty" meant the strength, courage, bellicosity, and candor regarded in antiquity as "virtues"; "base," "mediocre," and "the herd" are synonyms designating the cowardly and feeble majority. In the age when the aristocracy held the power of designation, they conceived the concept of "good" out of the perception of themselves and the satisfaction in their own strength, while those who appeared mediocre under the sway of power (weak in body and character; mediocre in physiology and psychology) were seen by the noble as "unfortunate." Within "misfortune" and "deficiency" lies the embryo of "evil." In the ancient Greek and Roman world — Nietzsche's constant source of inspiration, praised as "glory and greatness" — "good" and "bad," "good" and "evil" correspond respectively to the master (aristocrat) and slave (populace) classes. This elitist aristocratic morality is precisely the value system of the classical world, and Nietzsche's ideal ethical system: it is based purely on strength. In the view of master morality, that the strong rule the weak and the powerful oppress the powerless is natural, and because natural, also just. For this very reason, keeping slaves was then ethical. Priority in physiology, priority in politics, and priority of soul are identical. If the slave does not submit to this morality, it likewise leads to no moral difficulty: he must himself become that strong one, become the master. Master morality is entirely a kingdom of strength, which accords with the core of Nietzsche's philosophy: it best embodies the operation of the will to power.

Opposed to master morality is slave morality, which is also the morality of the weak, the morality of the populace, Christian morality — and yet this morality is the victor of the two in their historical struggle. Nietzsche declares that slave morality comes from the Jews, and is in essence the hatred bred by the powerlessness of the weak. In this second morality, the causal chain of the genealogy operates thus: the "herd," opposed to the aristocracy, feels powerless and lowly (Nietzsche regards inferiority as objective); and out of the inability to resist, breeds hatred toward the strong; and finally, they externalize this hatred into an idea transcending themselves, worship it, and take this illusory thing born of a lie as a spiritual weapon against real strength, against aristocratic morality. Christianity, then, is "the slave revolt in morality": "It was the Jews who, in opposition to the aristocratic value-equation (good = noble = powerful = beautiful = happy = beloved of God), dared with a terrifying logic to invert it, and held fast to that inversion with the teeth of an abysmal hatred — namely, 'only the wretched are the good; only the poor, the powerless, the lowly are the good; and only the suffering, the deprived, the sick, the ugly are the pious, are those blessed by God, for whom alone blessedness exists — while you, you noble and violent ones, are for all eternity the evil, the cruel, the greedy, the insatiable, the godless; you shall forever be the unblessed, the accursed, and the damned!'" In the rise of Christianity, the most fundamental value judgment was inverted: powerlessness and weakness, by way of "compassion," replaced nobility and strength as the highest value.

So how did the inversion come about? In other words, what gave slave morality the strength to accomplish its overthrow? "Love." Nietzsche holds that Christian love is the most hypocritical, most deceptive, and most clever — in a certain sense also the most powerful — invention of slave morality. The "love" expressed through the mouth of Christ is far cleverer than the commandments issued by the cruel and cold Jehovah of the Old Testament; "love" won Christianity an unprecedentedly broad body of believers — in Nietzsche's words, "love" enabled the Jews to poison and abduct the world. "Love" concerns the City of Man and the City of God. The care between human beings — "love thy neighbor as thyself" — compassion for and shelter of the weak, and the encouragement of doing good, constitute the law of the human world; while the need to rely on God for grace further strengthens the externality and authority of the moral judgments above. Earth and the kingdom of heaven (corresponding exactly to the inner and outer aspects of Christian morality) are, through "love," tightly bound and jointly governed. Yet Nietzsche points out that Christian love is hypocritical in at least two respects. First, through the doctrine of the transmigration of souls, and by dressing up physiological and psychological weakness as virtue, Christianity falsely presents the truth of the herd's inability to attain happiness as a noble choice — he is capable of happiness, but the pious one follows in the footsteps of Jesus and makes the right choice. He chooses to do good to others and to bear suffering himself in order to please God and obtain God's grace, for the sake of the afterlife, for a higher happiness on the far shore. Such self-deception is like self-consolation: "one only beats the dog one loves best." Second, in its doctrine — especially in the promise of the Last Judgment — the "love" Christianity proclaims reveals the undimmed base color of its hatred. "God commands that all in high places be honored" is not meant to defer the revenge upon the strong that the weak are in fact powerless to carry out, but rather that on the day of the Last Judgment there will await them the hell and purgatorial tortures they deserve, a thousand times crueler than any revenge in this life. How could a religion that proclaims "love" everywhere have the imagination and the interest to invent so many sensational and elaborate tortures? Because it is fundamentally hypocritical. On the contrary, the strong, by virtue of their powerful vitality, can be broad-minded, and truly capable of love.

Nietzsche holds that the most fundamental negative effect of slave morality's inversion of master morality is that it leads to nihilism. By promoting value judgments favorable to the survival of the weak, slave morality tames the strong and causes the degeneration of humanity as a whole. Nihilism means rejection of and indifference to human strength, capacity, and will, a loss of interest and confidence in humankind — that is, anti–will to power.

And yet, looking back over Nietzsche's genealogy of morals, we are not hard-pressed to find various problems. Rather than saying master morality is superior for its historical currency, it would be better to say it is primitive. If doing good and asceticism are to be seen as hypocritical love, then the release of passion, the indulgence of spirit, and the annihilation or oppression of the weak can even less be "good"; if slave morality causes humanity to degenerate, then master morality is of even less benefit to humanity as a whole: bloody history might well have ended long ago in some bloodier massacre, and humankind could scarcely have continued to this day. On the other hand, declaring Christianity to be slave morality cannot explain the fact of its cruelty and mercilessness toward the weak: if, as slave morality, Christianity arose for the self-preservation and protection of the weak, then why were the actual targets of the Crusades and the Inquisition the still more helpless? — and those were events from the era when Christianity was at its zenith, not during its decline. The gravest problem is that Nietzsche only points out the moral problem, without giving an answer. After loudly proclaiming "God is dead" and casting off Christian morality, where is humanity to go? Can it only return to the nature of the "blond beast," to the age of iron and blood? What is certain is that resurrecting an ancient god and believing in it anew, replacing Jesus Christ with Dionysus, is by no means the direction of the modern. Reason might originally have been an answer, but Nietzsche denies it along with the rest. In *Twilight of the Idols*, he tries to strike the statues of the great sages and hear their hollow echo — it was precisely Socrates and Plato who led Athens into the world of reason, and Greek culture, whose vitality springs from tragedy, thereupon declined. If religion and reason are interrogated and rejected at the same time, then the sole remaining answer is too thin to be possible: strength. At the end of this book's first essay, "'Good and Evil,' 'Good and Bad,'" Nietzsche pins Europe's hope of escaping the predicament of slave morality on Napoleon. But this "synthesis of the inhuman and the superhuman" could not even save France. Just as people have already walked out of the Middle Ages, history left the age of heroes long ago.
