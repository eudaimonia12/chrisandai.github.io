Last year, over the Qingming holiday,

it was already afternoon when I suggested to a friend that we climb to the summit of Victoria Peak, and past a quarter past six when we set off from the shops in Central. Because I had walked up the mountain with ease in the winter of 2018 and fancied I still had some lingering sense of the route, and because the evening light was still ample, an impromptu hike seemed, at that moment, feasible. But in the blurred rain and mist, we soon lost our way among countless steps and paths. The same flight of steps rose straight upward; I counted two hundred and seven, he said two hundred and fourteen. Before the streetlights came on, the banyan trees — vast as the old rulers of the place — could not be seen clearly; drawing near, I found one squatting on deep, thick roots by the side of the road, its dangling aerial roots covering most of a retaining wall, and I thought again of the image of fungus-covered walls in *The Last of Us*. In the third decade of the twenty-first century, in this city, what a mountain climb calls to mind should be this — accompanied by memories of popular culture, rather than by lines of verse that describe it far more accurately, such as "a hundred steps and nine turns winding round crag and peak" or "how many towers and terraces stand in the misty rain." Hong Kong Island is so bizarre and dazzling; reality is a door left ajar, and one gust of wind can release many fantasies, an experience full of heterogeneity.

We left the main road; the steps before us were marked "For Slope Maintenance Only," and Apple Maps directed us onward — I did not know then that this unfamiliar guide would lead me wandering through an unfamiliar world, while Amap lost its place on my device — but a running-in period was needed after all. The winding path led into seclusion, and soon there was no way through at all; the concrete footprints of civilization vanished, and only a broken flagstone path was faintly discernible, taking us around rocks, rotten wood, and tree roots. In late April, everything south of the Yangtze was raining. The unbroken drizzle was the inescapable backdrop of this trip; it never reached the point of requiring an umbrella, but as we climbed, moisture accumulated in the hills and the ground grew ever more slippery. When the twilight was spent and I could not tell which of the two forks before me was the less traveled, I realized we had no choice but to retrace our steps. Night fell, and the curiosity of exploration and the leisurely pace were quickly replaced by a pressing, oppressive darkness — and, if I'm not ashamed to admit it, by fear; anyone who has walked mountain paths at night has probably felt this. For a moment I wanted nothing else, my mind wholly on lights, transport, and food — in other words, human civilization. The destination changed from Victoria Peak to the nearest metro station. The nearest metro station was the University of Hong Kong.

The main road we had been on was called Po Shan Road; after walking a stretch back along it, the navigation led us to a gentle downhill path, and we were at last on the right track, though threading through unlit hills was still a heart-stopping journey. Hearing the gurgle of water in the darkness, I realized we were crossing a waterfall, and the path before us was in fact a bridge, with the dark on either side waiting for us to fall. By the time my eyes adjusted to the dark, a signpost pointing toward the campus was already before us, and the stairway leading all the way down was reassuring. There were no walls, sentry posts, or gatekeepers — none of the clues that make a school into the prison I'm used to; from the mountain path one could arrive directly behind a teaching building. We walked down at our ease, while students speaking Korean smoked at their ease on the last few steps. Only after returning and turning to my books did I discover that today's dead-end adventure had coincidentally overlapped with the route Eileen Chang once took to class at HKU: from 1939 to 1941, she set out from the St. Mary's women's dormitory at No. 8 Po Shan Road, passing along Kotewall Road and University Drive, to attend classes each day in the Main Building. This route was verified by Professor Nicole Huang of HKU; scattered descriptions of the mountain path appear in Eileen Chang's fiction and essays, especially *Jasmine Tea*, *Little Reunions*, and *The Book of Change*, all of which I had long known well before setting foot there — yet walking the whole way in reality, I had no idea at all, and the marvel of fate thrilled me so that I beat my chest and stamped my feet.

In *Jasmine Tea* she writes:

> "The mountain path turned a corner and suddenly opened out, revealing the whole of the sky and sea. Beside the road was a flat patch of hillside hanging in midair, ringed by a semicircle of iron railing. Chuanqing was walking ahead; he turned his head and, not seeing Danzhu behind him, looked again — and there she was, leaning on the railing. At the foot of the cliff the pines surged and roared, and there was a hardy kind of tree whose leaves were green on one side and white on the other. The great wind swallowed them, and the leaves all over the mountain heaved and tumbled, so that one saw only points of silver light scattering everywhere. Where the clouds broke, the pale-yellow winter moon came out, and the whitened sky and sea spread behind Danzhu a folding screen of mica."

That night, the bay was buried beneath layers of fog, and there was no mica-pale moon either, but it was just as cold and still, arousing two opposite passions: to return to a warm, dry indoors, or to keep waiting for something about to happen.

I ate dinner in the HKU canteen. Just as her student-era Hong Kong experience was the wellspring of Eileen Chang's writing, my own university years are seared into me, and any writing of the self cannot get around them. To this day I still often dream of eating those same two or three dishes in the canteen, then buying yogurt at the campus supermarket — in the dream I know that I have already graduated and lost my campus card, so how am I to pay? A moment's panic and I wake. Now, in a campus that is not mine, I get to walk peacefully into the dream (the self-service ordering machine takes Alipay); the char siu at the HKU canteen was especially satisfying, and I could taste a fine plate of scallion-oil chicken with roast duck over rice. For any adventure, this counts as a grand finale.
