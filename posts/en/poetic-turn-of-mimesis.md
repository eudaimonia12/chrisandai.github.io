*Abstract.* "Mimesis" is an important concept in ancient Greek philosophy. Its verb form, *mimeisthai*, was widely used by Plato and became the core of the doctrine of separation in his metaphysics; on the other hand, "mimesis" was also the earliest theory and aesthetic standard applied to the aesthetic domain in Greece — ancient Greek art was called "mimetic art," and Plato critiqued works of art on the basis of "the mimesis theory." In Aristotle's *Poetics*, "the mimesis theory" underwent a major turn: its object and its interest broke free from the bonds of metaphysics and ethics, and turned instead to art and literature, becoming "artistic mimesis," an independent and complete aesthetic theory.

To trace the origin of "mimesis," like the pre-Socratic philosophers' inquiry into the origin of the world, is to attend to a fundamental topic: nature. Nature is the way the world presents itself — but what, exactly, is this way? Is it a substance: water, fire, the "boundless"? Or a state: eternal, regular motion, or absolutely perfect stillness? While the natural philosophers each put forth their own views, trying to explain the origin of the world, another aspect of the same topic arose: what is the relation between nature and man?

For the ancient Greeks, the answer to this basic question was "mimesis." Aristotle even attributed the fundamental role of mimetic activity in human life to human instinct. To understand "mimesis" correctly in the ancient Greek conception, one must first note the many ways this concept differs from the same word in everyday language. First, the subject and object of mimesis do not present the dualistic relation they do today. Mimesis is not deliberate imitation; it contains in itself no subjective intention. The act of mimesis is itself natural; only when it is generalized and refined into theory and concept, and analyzed and discussed, is mimesis conscious, and only then does the act begin to contain reflection. In the ancient Greek conception, there is no rupture among nature, gods, and man; on the contrary, they are unified in a monism of nature: "The world of ideas of the Greeks was entirely free of the influence of dualism; in the eyes of the Greeks, all parts of the world were homogeneous." For this very reason, that gods and men share the same form and nature, that human affairs imitate myth, and that artifacts represent natural things, had long been seen as natural, and therefore as the legitimate and only mode of existence, and was widely accepted; while the critiques of Xenophanes and Plato were the results of deep reflection. Second, the subject of mimesis is subject to no restriction: mimesis refers not to some particular act of man, but includes all acts; not only can humans, human activities, and artifacts carry out mimesis, but all concrete, perceptible things may be regarded as imitations.

In the pre-Aristotelian era, the concept of "mimesis" had already received interpretation, whether profound or broad, in the fields of metaphysics and naive sociology. When the Pythagorean school established "number" as the origin of all things, it implied the imitation by concrete things of the "mathematical archetype" or "proportion" behind them; in Plato's doctrine of separation, the perceptible world and the world of Ideas obtained a clear distinction, and the way the two worlds are connected is precisely mimesis. In fact, *mimeisthai* appears in almost all of Plato's works. *The Republic* first points out that the changeable and imperfect essence and attributes that concrete things display are imitations of the eternal Ideas behind them; in the *Symposium* and the *Sophist*, the highest love and wisdom are, respectively, imitations of "beauty" and truth; the *Timaeus*, through the form of myth, defines creation as a mimetic activity: first there is the absolutely perfect Idea, and then the demiurge creates, according to the Idea, a "divine imitation" — nature. Plato's ontology presents a clear causal chain of "Idea — natural thing — artifact"; for any pair of these concepts, the former is the origin of the latter, and the latter is an imitation of the former. At the same time, the word "mimesis" also attained a self-consciousness within the field of social science; the ancient Greeks used mimesis to describe their technical-practical activities. The great physician Hippocrates, in the fifth century BC, summarized in some detail the mimetic relation of technique to nature: "The generation and formation of the arts is the result of inspiration from nature; in other words, the arts arise as an imitation of natural phenomena and their processes of operation." Mimesis is not confined to the copy; it also has a completing and supplementing counter-effect upon nature: "Art assists the work of nature and helps nature realize its own aims. The art of medicine arose in response to the needs of life, and developed along the process of cooperation with nature. Through diagnosis, the physician first ascertains the condition, then prescribes accordingly, restoring the body's lost balance." At the same time, the rich vase painting, painting, and sculpture of ancient Greece are, for the most part, concrete depictions of nature. Because the Greek conception held to a "monism of nature" — nature being beautiful and sublime — the meticulous imitation and direct presentation of nature constituted the ideal of Greek visual art. Greek "mimetic art" emphasized verisimilitude and simplicity, and rejected violent romanticism and exaggeration, ultimately presenting "a noble simplicity and a quiet grandeur in gesture and expression." One can see that attention to nature and emphasis on technique jointly stimulated people's mimetic activity, and that mimesis theories in different fields followed close behind to explain the practice. Yet such theory was not yet sufficient: even though the thought of the ancient sages had already touched on "beauty" and art, before Aristotle a genuine Greek theory of beauty was still absent, and the potential of "the mimesis theory" to become an aesthetic theory had not yet been realized. The reasons include, of course, the lag of theory behind practice, but the fundamental reason lies in the blurring of art and technique.

A clear distinction between art and technique did not occur in ancient Greece; craft, technique, and art were uniformly designated by the single word *Tekhnai*. *Tekhnai* is the summing-up of all experience or knowledge produced by human making, while human making corresponds to nature's spontaneous growth and generation. Therefore, like technique, art was often treated and discussed as a kind of "art of making." It is worth noting that the notion of imitating nature has a profound inner connection with the blurring of technique and art. First, curiosity about nature and the cultural psychology of actively imitating nature together underlay the Greek emphasis on technique, producing the various crafts, including art; second, because it was likewise regarded as an imitation of nature, art could not be independent of technique and appreciated and studied as a genuine aesthetic object. In taking nature as the fundamental object of imitation, the mimetic conception in effect also granted nature the status of the fundamental aesthetic object. Therefore, the fundamental measure by which ancient Greece made aesthetic judgments of art was verisimilitude. Art, which at most "approximates" nature, must necessarily be inferior to nature, and also inferior to "real" knowledge and truth. It is precisely in this sense that Plato advanced, from a practical standpoint, both an ethical and a metaphysical critique of art: (1) art arouses people's desires, to the detriment of the harmony of the city-state; (2) the work of art is a copy of a copy, three removes from truth. Art can only imitate appearances, and stands at the very bottom in the order of verisimilitude. For these reasons, the poet is the enemy of the philosopher, and there is no place for the poet in the "ideal state." It must be seen that Plato's censure of art, with respect to the aesthetic object, is entirely imposed; but a critique made on grounds of utility is not inappropriate to a mimetic technique that stresses "verisimilitude." Therefore, to regard art with an aesthetic eye requires the emergence of a concept of art independent of technique. Aristotle's creative inheritance of "the mimesis theory" completed the "poetic turn" of the mimetic conception, and endowed Greek aesthetic theory with its initial independence and completeness.

Compared with the traditional mimetic conception, Aristotle made a meticulous distinction within the concept of "mimesis," whereby "technical mimesis" and "artistic mimesis" became clearly separate. The two differ in object: technique imitates nature, while art imitates life. They also differ in interest: the *Physics* discusses the relation of technique and nature, highlighting the productive nature of technique; while the *Poetics* focuses on the discussion of the technique of poetry, belonging to aesthetic study. Therefore, artistic mimesis excludes manual technique, and no longer stands under a judgment of practical value. By maintaining that form is immanent in existing bodies, refuting Plato's doctrine of separation, Aristotle rescued art from the ontological-hierarchical critique of metaphysics. At this point, art has basically shaken off Plato's censure. At the same time, Aristotle defined poetry as the art that "imitates by means of language alone," and took tragedy among its kinds as the object of the study of the poetic art. This choice may be because the objects tragedy depicts are heroes and nobles, which is superior to comedy's general depiction of mediocre and base persons, and better represents the lofty spirit of Greek art; the more direct reason is that, in Aristotle's era, tragedy had already become a perfected art: "Tragedy grew up slowly; each time a new element appeared, the poet improved upon it, and after many transformations, it ceased to develop once it had attained its natural attributes." Compared with the visual arts, which depend on technique and craft (and are therefore easily equated with craft), poetry is an art that relies more purely on inspiration. Through the study of the poetic art, Aristotle refuted the theory of divinely bestowed inspiration held by Plato and others, making serious study of art possible. In sum, it is precisely in Aristotle's "poetic turn" that art was able to become independent of technique, and the seed of a genuine aesthetic theory was produced.

The "theory of artistic mimesis" granted art an unprecedentedly lofty new status: art imitates life, and should be higher than life. But if it is an imitation of life, why can art be higher than life? Because for Aristotle, the standard of value judgment had already shifted from the "principle of verisimilitude" to the "principle of universality." Only when verisimilitude is the measure is art inferior to its object of imitation; but "the poet's task lies not in describing what has happened, but in describing what could happen" — that is, "that which is of a universal nature." By virtue of the principle of universality, what art expresses is not the appearance of concrete things, but the infinite possibilities that transcend things, and life refined; it should therefore naturally attain a status higher than life and concrete existents. The more far-reaching significance of this principle is that the demand for universality forced "mimetic art" later to confront its own limitations, thereby inspiring the emergence of abstract and symbolic art. The value of art is also reflected in its function: "(Tragedy), by arousing pity and fear, brings about the catharsis of these emotions." Unlike Plato, who held that art arouses harmful desires, Aristotle, on the basis of affirming that art promotes passion, further discovered its function of channeling emotion. Empathizing with the characters in a work of art, the audience is able to release emotions that are too violent, and finally attain a state of quiet elegance.

Admittedly, even after the independence and poetic turn of "artistic mimesis," and the affirmation that art has a value higher than life, Aristotle's theory of art still cannot escape the limitations of its era and its materials. Bosanquet summarized his aesthetic thought thus:

> Aristotle not only retained the term "mimetic," regarding it as the characteristic of art, without qualifying this "mimetic" in the scientific vocabulary that admits idealization; but, more importantly, on the question of by what principle the direction of this idealization is to be determined, he gave no clear answer.

On this point of "the mimesis theory" we cannot, and need not, defend Aristotle; no one can easily deny within himself the cultural tradition in which he is steeped. But Aristotle did indeed point to an ideal paradigm of art, namely universality. In this sense, Aristotle made an important contribution to the history of aesthetics, linking what came before with what came after.
