August 11, 2023: this is my 872nd day working at Specialized, and my last.

872 days have flown by. Over two and a half years, events large and small came at me with a density that left no time to catch my breath, forming a shared memory that was unforgettable, full, and bittersweet all at once. History moved into the fast lane; time seemed to be breaking through a siege, and amid all the clamor and confusion our small, lovable industry underwent upheaval too, and unexpectedly entered a golden age. Whether one sees it as luck or as inevitability, compared with three years ago there are now millions of people beginning to enjoy the pleasure of cycling, for whom riding has become part of daily life, shaping their lives in a joyful and healthy way. I'm sure you have family and friends like this around you; or perhaps you are one of them yourself. And I believe that, as a company that creates fun, Specialized — that is, all of us — has a share in that joy of riding.

I remember that not long ago, when the company was somewhat smaller, a departing colleague would write a few words for everyone, and for themselves. I have great fondness for this now slightly old-fashioned custom. In form, the farewell letter makes an exquisite symmetry with the newcomer's announcement: a hello answered by a goodbye, a self-introduction by a personal summing-up, the job description by the destination one departs for; and the excitement, anticipation, and unease of joining give way, at the moment of parting, to gratitude, regret, and reluctance to leave. My favorite writer compared life to a thread of light between two eternities of darkness; in that sense, a job is precisely the line of writing traced between a welcome letter and a farewell letter. Between those final words, one ought to be able to see the change and growth the job has brought.

A stretch of work is like a group ride. We always set out alone, and later meet groups large and small along the road, matching our pace; and so there comes acceptance and belonging, rapport and friendship, division of labor and strategy. Riding in a group, we share a direction or a goal, with colleagues before and behind and to either side, and can ride more easily and reach farther places. We also have to be attentive to and take care of one another — to value others' feelings, needs, and stamina — if we are to keep each other company for the long haul. And sometimes, when we find that our rhythm no longer matches the group's, or that there is a route we would rather explore, we have no choice but to leave the team: some accelerate off alone, some break away in the opposite direction, some go looking for new companions. By this analogy, I would say this has been a group ride I truly enjoyed, and I'm very grateful to have had you along. Looking back on the road we came by, we've already come a long way. Everyone rode wonderfully.

Now I've come again to a fork in the road, and again I've chosen the one less traveled. In doing so, I will break away entirely from the trajectory of my adult life and seek a new fate in a foreign land, as though stepping into a parallel universe. This means leaving and farewell, but it also makes possible the thrill of reunion: as the stars wheel around, in another time and space, our paths will cross again, overlap, and part. Of course there will be sadness. But I'll remind myself that today's sadness is precisely because of yesterday's beauty. So hold tight to the beautiful parts.

Bring water. Keep in touch. And I'll see you on the road.
