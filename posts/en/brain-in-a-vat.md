At the opening of *Reason, Truth and History*, Hilary Putnam gives a relatively complete description of the thought experiment of the "brain in a vat":

> "Imagine that a human being has been subjected to an operation by an evil scientist. This person's brain has been removed from the body and placed in a vat of nutrients that keeps it alive. The nerve endings have been connected to a super-scientific computer that gives the owner of the brain the illusion that everything is perfectly normal. There seem to be people, objects, the sky, and so on; but in fact all that this person experiences is the result of electronic impulses transmitted from the computer to the nerve endings. This computer is so clever that if the person tries to raise a hand, the feedback from the computer will make him 'see' and 'feel' the hand being raised. Moreover, by varying the program, the evil scientist can cause the victim to 'experience' (that is, hallucinate) any situation or environment the evil scientist wishes. He can also erase the traces of the brain operation, so that the victim will feel that he has always been in this environment. This victim may even suppose that he is sitting reading a book — reading precisely such an interesting but utterly absurd supposition: that an evil scientist removed a human brain from the body and placed it in a vat of nutrients to keep it alive, the nerve endings supposedly connected to a super-scientific computer, and that he is the owner of this brain, holding such-and-such illusions…"

In this scenario, the most central question — and the most intriguing, perhaps even the original reason people invented this thought experiment — is: can a brain in a vat realize that it is a brain in a vat? Putnam holds that the answer is no. His argument is this: although the brain in a vat has intelligence and cognitive capacity equal to other people's, enough to produce (in him? in it?) images similar to an ordinary person's, he has no non-linguistic input and output with the external world; in short, he has no physical connection to the outside, and therefore no concept within the brain in a vat has any capacity for reference. That is to say, even if the brain in a vat, by accident or for some other unknown reason, thinks of or utters the phrase "brain in a vat," that phrase must necessarily differ from the concept "brain in a vat" that we are discussing, because it lacks the referential capacity the latter possesses. In sum, a person (or a thing) cannot represent something entirely outside his experience, because he has no genuine cognition of it.

So what is genuine cognition? Putnam (it seems) claims that there must exist a causal chain involving non-linguistic input and output. I must have touched an apple, felt the stimulation of its red skin on sight and touch; or I must have tasted it, be familiar with its texture — now crisp, now mealy — and the flavor, perhaps sour, perhaps sweet, and how my taste buds respond. A fuller precondition would be that I have both eaten and touched apples, and planted the tree, and picked the fruit — in short, entered into physical connections of every category with the apple. The second link of the causal chain is that, through these prior physical connections, I spontaneously produce the image of "apple." The requirement that immediately follows is that I must, like everyone else, use the word "apple" within the scope of daily life and in the overall sense of human culture (setting aside individual, idiosyncratic cases — for instance, "you are my little apple" is a referential meaning peculiar to contemporary Chinese pop culture). Only in this sense do I "know" what an apple is, and only then does the concept of apple in my mind possess the function of referring to the real apple. By contrast, a Turing-test machine that can converse freely with a human on the topic of "apple" and recite the online encyclopedia entry for "apple" backwards is merely a fraud who can only meet sentences with sentences, and in fact knows nothing about apples. The "brain in a vat," in the same sense, knows nothing about the thought experiment we have envisioned, and therefore cannot use "brain in a vat" in the same sense we do, and so cannot realize that it is the "brain in a vat" of which we speak.

But from the conclusion above — that the "brain in a vat" cannot realize it is a "brain in a vat" — can we infer that "we, who designed the thought experiment of the 'brain in a vat,' are not brains in a vat"? I think we can. Being able to conceive of the "brain in a vat," and to create *Westworld*, suffices to prove that these concepts are not outside our world, and therefore that we are neither "brains in a vat" nor living in some game-like "modern world." But this inference is not enough to prove the reality of the world. The only thing that is real is this: the real world is not false in the way those within it imagine it to be. That is the boundary of the real.
