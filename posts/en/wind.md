🌬 Unlike the smog, snowfall, or sandstorms of other places, the strong winds of spring in Shanghai are not considered any kind of representative or special weather. Once the windy season has passed, people, recalling it, always feel it was as mild as the city's climate on the whole.

And yet, in March and April, the wind is at its height — young and vigorous, strong enough to grip the whole city in its palm. Two years ago I rode a road bike set to a relaxed gearing through a full Shanghai spring, thirty kilometers a day. What followed was a lower-back pain so bad I couldn't sit or stand still, sharp enough to bead sweat on my forehead. Young as I am, how could I have back pain? The bike and I had been on perfectly good terms for two years. Turning it over and over, the only explanation is the wind.

Shanghai's wind is not physical damage but magic damage. It strikes on the warm, blossoming days, disarms your guard, and then turns into a feline creature toying with its prey.
