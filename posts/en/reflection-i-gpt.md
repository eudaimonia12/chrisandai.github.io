Twelve years ago, Siri was released alongside the iPhone 4s. Just starting seventh grade, I was full of anticipation: this heavily promoted AI voice assistant would chat and laugh with us, meet us mind to mind, help us find the information we wanted and carry out the functions we needed. Just as ChatGPT does today.

As it turned out, Siri — which by age would also be starting seventh grade this year — became a feature of dubious use. Early this morning, the sun was bright, and being stuck in heavy traffic put me in a good mood; I suddenly very much wanted to hear "Another Day of Sun," the opening song of *La La Land*. And yet Siri could not fulfill my request, though I articulated it clearly, with a full breath, concisely, and even politely. I thought of a story called "The Lament for Zhongyong," and felt deeply disappointed.

Perhaps this year is another "year one." Perhaps OpenAI and its succession of GPTs can make language-based AI genuinely usable, and thereby change the experience of life for many people. Still, as a child who grew up alongside concepts like artificial intelligence and deep learning, I think the progress of my own intelligence has the slight edge over these artificial ones. At the same time, people are pouring untold resources and capital into AI technology, and I don't think the development of human intelligence — a.k.a. education — has received equal attention. The new generation of internet natives build their knowledge, desires, and skills out of a vast ocean of data. Rather like a pack of untended wild AIs roaming the databases.

I'm not worried about a future in which humanity is ruled by AI. I'm worried that we are too short-sighted, too greedy, too selfish — so much so that we won't live to see that day.
