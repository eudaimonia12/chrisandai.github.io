Perhaps contrary to the impression of some, Kafka's texts do not merely point out the absurd, bleak picture of the world people find themselves in; they also offer a so-called "solution." Of course, the solution here is only a refuge, not an exit — because the world has no exit, and those great problems have only theoretical, not real, solutions.

Kafka's friend and literary executor Max Brod: "So then, is there hope outside this manifestation of the world we know?"

He (Kafka) smiled: "Oh, plenty of hope, an infinite amount of hope — only not for us."

So to whom does hope belong? Kafka's answer is: children, adolescents, fools, simpletons, clowns, and other innocents who have not been fully disciplined. Transposed into the Chinese context, that means the madman of *A Madman's Diary* and the children of its cry, "Save the children." Reason and calculation will be defeated again and again, and their end is despair and nothingness; but to give up scheming and reckoning, to give up thinking, and to retreat into the trivial — yet, precisely because of their humbleness, indelible — pleasures of life is to raise a shield against mediocrity and violence.

This may be too pessimistic and passive an attitude; you could also criticize it as muddling through one's days in a daze. I don't know. Because awakening is really quite easy, and yet this world leaves no space for the things an awakened person would have to do.
